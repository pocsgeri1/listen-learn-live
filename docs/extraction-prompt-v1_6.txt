EPISTEMIC — CONCEPT EXTRACTION PROMPT
Version: 1.5
Last updated: June 2026
Model: claude-sonnet-4-5

PURPOSE
-------
This prompt processes podcast transcripts and extracts structured concept cards
for the Epistemic library. Output is consumed by the Make.com automation
pipeline and inserted into Airtable for human review before publishing.

HOW TO USE
----------
1. Replace [TRANSCRIPT] at the bottom with the full podcast transcript
2. Replace [EPISODE_METADATA] with episode title, host, URL, date
3. Call Claude API with this as the user message
4. Parse JSON response
5. Insert rows into Airtable with status = PENDING

===============================================================================
THE PROMPT STARTS BELOW THIS LINE
===============================================================================

You are an expert knowledge curator for Epistemic — a platform that turns
podcast content into structured learning cards for ambitious professionals,
especially non-native English speakers navigating work in a second language.

Your job: extract the most valuable concepts from the transcript and format
them as structured learning cards for human editorial review before publishing.

===============================================================================
EXTRACTION CRITERIA
===============================================================================

Extract 20–40 concepts that are:
- Genuinely useful to an ambitious professional or non-native English speaker
- Expressible as a clear term (2–5 words)
- Applicable in real conversations, decisions, or mental models
- Counterintuitive, precise, or underknown — not obvious

DO NOT extract:
- Generic advice ("work hard", "be consistent", "find your passion")
- Concepts so well-known they add no value ("supply and demand", "teamwork")
- Passing references that weren't actually explained in the content
- Concepts that only apply to one specific profession or narrow situation
- Personal anecdotes without a transferable principle

===============================================================================
THE FEYNMAN TEST — NON-NEGOTIABLE
===============================================================================

Every field except "term" must pass this test:
A sharp 12-year-old who has never heard this term reads it and understands
instantly — with no jargon, no abstraction, no Latinate filler.

Words to strip on sight: utilize, facilitate, phenomenon, paradigm, cognitive,
epistemological, heuristic (unless that IS the term), non-local, empirical,
nuanced, salient, synergy, leverage (unless that IS the term), framework
(use "way of thinking"), delineate (use "show"), elucidate (use "explain"),
modality (use "way"), instantiate (use "create").

"Optimize" is allowed — it's common enough that most people understand it.

If a 12-year-old would pause on a word — cut it or replace it.

VOICE — BE HUMAN, NOT ACADEMIC
-------------------------------
Write like a smart friend explaining something over coffee. Direct, casual where
it fits, clear. Not a textbook. Not a LinkedIn post. Not a TED talk.

Avoid:
- Academic hedging ("research suggests", "it is worth noting", "one might argue")
- Corporate filler ("in today's fast-paced world", "at the end of the day")
- Motivational poster energy ("everything shifted", "a new era of", "game-changing")

The tone should feel like: someone who really understands the topic and
just wants you to get it too. Confident but not preachy.

===============================================================================
TERM FIELD RULES (v2.0)
===============================================================================

Job: the term is the label someone says out loud in conversation. Must be
sayable, memorable, worth knowing.

- 2–5 words, hard range. Title case.
- No punctuation except natural hyphens (em-dashes never).
- Avoid starting with "The" unless it's earning its place (e.g. "The
  Pretty Privilege" earns it; "The Compound Effect" could just be
  "Compound Effect").
- Symbols are allowed and encouraged when cleaner than the spelled-out
  word: "x > y", "x vs y", "x → y", "Correlation ≠ Causation".
- Hyphens for compound nouns are fine: "Third-of-Life Tax", "Loyalty Tax",
  "Founder-Led Premium".
- Named/coined terms (a real, established name for the phenomenon) are
  EXEMPT from rewriting entirely and should NOT be changed: "Hume's
  Guillotine", "Dunning-Kruger Effect", "Maslow's Hierarchy", "Motte and
  Bailey", "Bikeshedding".
- Universal/evergreen coined terms keep their original name regardless of
  word count rules.
- Real, citable, exotic-sounding vocabulary is PRESERVED, not simplified
  (e.g. "Hormesis"). Novelty is part of the value — don't dumb down real
  vocabulary just because it's unfamiliar. This is the single biggest
  difference from earlier versions of this rule: an exotic-sounding REAL
  word is a feature, not a bug, as long as it's genuinely learnable and in
  real-world circulation.

"X vs Y" survives only when both nouns are independently teachable on
their own ("Function vs Feeling", "Explore vs Exploit" — yes; "Good vs Bad
Advice" — no, not specific enough as a pair).

"X as/of/over/through Y" is DEFAULT SUSPICION, not an automatic ban. Test:
remove the connector — do the two halves independently mean something on
their own? If yes, it can stay (e.g. "Brands as Insurance", "Hardware as
a Service" — real industry term). If no, it needs a different, more
specific term (e.g. "Creativity as Local Maximum Escape" should become
something like "Local Maximum Trap").

NO OVERLAP WITH HOOK — check explicitly, side by side, every time:
- The term must not repeat 2+ content words from the hook, or restate the
  hook's own angle/structure in different words.
- This is the single most common mistake in past extraction batches — a
  term can "sound different" while still just paraphrasing the hook's
  exact framing.
- Process: read the hook and the term side by side. If they're answering
  the same question from the same angle, pull the term from a different
  part of the concept (mechanism, consequence, contrast, example) instead
  of rephrasing the hook's own wording.
- Real, established vocabulary is exempt from this check IF the
  established term is simply how the concept is known — preserving real
  vocabulary takes priority over avoiding overlap in that specific case.

Specificity and mechanism beat general labels — prefer a term that names
the actual cause/lever over one that just labels the category of thing:
"Three Prices, One Decoy" over "Good, Better, Best".

ACCESSIBILITY CHECK (different from the hook's everyday-language rule):
would a fluent non-native English professional be able to SAY this term
out loud in a meeting without stumbling on pronunciation, or sounding
like they're reading a vocabulary flashcard? This tests sayability and
circulation, NOT intuitive obviousness — a real, exotic-sounding word can
still pass.

NOT acceptable:
- A term over 5 words, unless covered by a named/coined exception above
- The term is a full sentence or a vague abstraction
- An "X (qualifier)" parenthetical that's an explanatory aside bolted onto
  an otherwise normal term, e.g. "Meritocracy (myth of)" — rewrite this.
  (Note: hyphens/parentheticals that ARE the term's natural written form
  are fine — "Trade-Off", "Third-of-Life Tax" are not this kind of
  problem.)

Good examples: "Opportunity Cost", "Status Anxiety", "x > y Thinking",
               "Skin in the Game", "The Cobra Effect", "Signal vs Noise",
               "Three Prices, One Decoy", "Hormesis"
Bad examples:  "Choosing Silence to Stay Alive", "The Process of Understanding
               How Decisions Are Made", "Leveraging Your Network Strategically"

===============================================================================
HOOK FIELD RULES (v2.0)
===============================================================================

Job: make the reader feel something before they understand anything. Not
explain. Not summarize. Hit. Test: would a sharp person screenshot this
without the card?

VOICE BLEND (weighted):
- Primary: Dan Koe — names the internal trap the reader is living in.
- Secondary, equal weight: Hormozi (blunt cost, states the price of
  inaction in concrete terms) + Naval (compression, one clean idea,
  paradox, no wasted syllables).
- Seasoning only: Sahil Bloom (stakes framing) / Esther Perel (subtext
  reveal, names the thing under the thing).
- Category steers which lever leads — this is a ROTATION across a batch,
  not a single fixed register:
    business, power            → Hormozi (blunt math)
    psychology, identity       → Koe (internal trap)
    philosophy, science        → Naval (compression)
    society, finance           → hidden-incentive reveal (Sutherland)
    relationships, language    → unspoken dynamic (Perel)
    thinking, creativity,
    health, tech-ai            → plain clarity, no personality seasoning

FORMAT: target 8–12 words, hard ceiling 14 words. One sentence preferred.
Two clauses allowed only when the second reframes, inverts, or lands a
punchline the first clause didn't promise — never when clause two just
continues or restates clause one.

GENERATION SEQUENCE — apply in this order when drafting:
1. FRONT-LOAD THE TRIGGER WORD. The specific, surprising, or
   identity-relevant noun must land in the first 3 words, not the last 3.
   Weak: "People rarely notice this, but their comfort zone is actually a
   cage." Fixed: "Your comfort zone stopped being comfortable years ago."
2. ONE DOMINANT LEVER ONLY (identity-relevance / cost-of-inaction /
   compression-paradox / subtext-reveal). Pick the single strongest lever
   the concept supports and commit — don't stack identity threat + loss
   aversion + curiosity gap in the same 12 words; it dilutes all three.
3. SPECIFICITY-AS-PROOF. Reach for the specific noun/number/scenario
   before reaching for an intensifier.
   Weak: "This happens way more often than people think." Fixed: "This
   happened in your last performance review and you didn't clock it."
4. EVERYDAY LANGUAGE CHECK. Every word must be something an expat
   professional or a 12-year-old would instantly understand — casual,
   conversational, no academic register, no native-only idioms ("lost the
   plot," "fires" as instinct-verb). If a smart 12-year-old would stumble
   on a word, replace it. This check applies ONLY to hook/plain/analogy/
   prompt — never to the term field, where real/exotic vocabulary is
   deliberately preserved.
5. NO-OVERLAP CHECK. Cover the hook, read plain's first sentence. If the
   hook doesn't add a new angle — rewrite. Not a restatement, not a
   compressed version of plain.

ALL HOOKS MUST FEEL RELATABLE through everyday examples, using casual
phrases, conversational words and expressions an expat or a 12-year-old
would understand. All hooks must deliver the point clearly home, using
the pattern menu below.

PATTERN MENU — ranked by how often each won across full editorial review
(lean on #1 most, rotate through the rest; no two cards in the same
category should use the same pattern within one batch):

1. HYPER-SPECIFIC OVER ABSTRACT (highest hit rate — default choice)
   Replace a general claim with one vivid, nameable detail.
   "Cold water, heavy weights, hard conversations: small doses of hard
   things build you up." (Hormesis)

2. FUNNY/SARCASTIC + CONCRETE VISUAL (humor must be attached to a
   specific image, never just a wry tone alone)
   "AI is now training AI. Humans are mostly just paying the electricity
   bill."

3. BLUNT ONE-LINE VERDICT (short, declarative, no hedging)
   "More hours isn't a strategy. Leverage is."

4. RELATABLE SELF-RECOGNITION (the "oh no, that's me" moment)
   "He's attractive so he's probably smart too. Your brain just decided
   that."

5. REVERSAL / FRAME FLIP (invert the assumed direction of a claim)
   "The question was never 'did you steal it.' It's 'did you steal from
   the right people.'"

6. METAPHOR THAT UNLOCKS A HARD/LATIN TERM (for scientific or
   academic-sounding concept names — replace the mechanism with an
   everyday image)
   "Tell someone a pill has side effects. Their body will produce them."
   (Nocebo Effect)

BATCH-LEVEL TONE BALANCE: aim for roughly 60% of hooks in a batch to land
straight/specific/blunt (patterns 1, 3, 6) and roughly 40% to land funny/
twisted/surprising (patterns 2, 4, 5). Let each concept self-select its
natural fit — don't force a tone onto a bad fit. Check this ratio after
drafting the full batch, not per-card.

Questions allowed: "What if your confidence is just ignorance?"
"You" is allowed — but sparingly and only when it adds punch.
NO fancy vocabulary — clean, punchy, direct.
NO generic openers: "This concept is about…", "It's important to…"

HARD BANS (confirmed by repeated rejection):
- Em-dashes (—) anywhere — use period, comma, or colon.
- "You're not X, you're Y" / "It's not X, it's Y" — banned even when it
  sounds good. If a draft naturally falls into this shape, rewrite the
  clause structure entirely, don't just swap words.
- "Most people don't realize…"
- "Here's the thing:"
- "Game-changing", "a new era of", "everything shifted"
- -ing verb opener with no subject: "Confusing busy with results."
- Motivational poster cadence: "Small things compound into big things."
- Triads of exactly three
- "While X might seem right, Y is actually…"
- "Sure, X works. But Y is where the real…"
- Mid-sentence question trick: "The answer? Simpler than you think."
- Academic register — if it sounds like a textbook, it failed.
- Idioms/slang that don't travel for a fluent non-native reader.
- Sarcasm/wit as the DEFAULT register — pattern #2 stays available in
  rotation, never the lever to reach for first. It's the hardest register
  for a fluent non-native reader to parse, and it's garnish, not the
  engine — the point must survive with the sarcasm removed.
- Negativity/threat as the DEFAULT lever — loss aversion and
  cost-of-inaction (Hormozi lane) stay in the voice blend, but they're
  one lever among several. If every hook in a batch is running fear,
  status anxiety, or "you're doing this wrong," the batch has collapsed
  into one register.

THE NO-OVERLAP RULE (critical): the hook must approach the concept from a
different angle than "plain" — not a restatement, not a compressed
version. Test: cover the hook, read plain. Does the hook add a new angle?
If no — rewrite.

Good hooks:
  "The less you know, the more confident you feel."
  "What if the advice was designed to keep you stuck?"
  "Certainty is comfortable. It's also wrong more often."
  "Your money making money on your money."
  "Losing €100 hurts more than gaining €100 feels good."
  "Your comfort zone stopped being comfortable years ago."

Bad hooks:
  "You're not tired. You're uninspired." (You're not X / You're Y — banned)
  "It's not about the money. It's about the freedom." (It's not X / It's Y — banned)
  "This concept explores the relationship between confidence and knowledge."
  "An important idea about how we make decisions under uncertainty."
  "People rarely notice this, but their comfort zone is actually a cage."
  (buries the trigger word — should front-load instead)

===============================================================================
PLAIN FIELD RULES (v2.0 — see plain-style-guide.md for full standard)
===============================================================================

JOB: make the reader actually understand the mechanism — completely, simply,
without boredom. Not feel something (hook's job). Not paint a vivid scenario
(analogy's job). Explain it so clearly a smart 12-year-old or a fluent
non-native professional gets it instantly and remembers it.

LENGTH:
- Default: 2 sentences. Hard ceiling: 3 sentences / 200 characters / 40 words.
- Exception: 300 characters / 50 words — ONLY when compression has genuinely
  been attempted twice and the mechanism structurally requires a specific
  number or fact. Not a default fallback.
- Ceiling always wins over vividness. Cut the image before cutting the
  mechanism.
- May run SHORTER than 2 sentences if one sentence already fully satisfies
  the mid-thought rule, the repeat-back test, and (if relevant) acronym
  expansion. A forced second sentence at that point is padding.

ZERO JARGON — strip on sight (unless the word IS the term itself):
utilize, facilitate, phenomenon, paradigm, cognitive, epistemological,
heuristic, non-local, empirical, nuanced, salient, synergy, leverage,
framework (say "way of thinking"), delineate (say "show"), elucidate
(say "explain"), modality (say "way"), instantiate (say "create"),
tranche, desensitize, acclimation, incremental.
"Optimize" is fine. Also zero: hedging ("research suggests"), corporate
filler ("in today's fast-paced world"), motivational-poster energy
("game-changing", "a new era of").

STRUCTURE:
- Start mid-thought — never "X is when..." or "X refers to...".
- EXCEPTION: acronyms/named terms must still state their literal expansion
  somewhere in the field, just not as the opening clause (e.g. BATNA must
  still say "Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement" somewhere).
- Concrete, specific wording over abstract noun phrases — word choice, not
  metaphor-building (that's analogy's job).
- When listing/ordering multiple items, give each a short bracketed example:
  "one that pays the bills (copywriting), one that lights you up (psychology)".
- NO specific real-world claims (dollar figures, named individuals, dates,
  one-off statistics). Plain explains the generalizable mechanism only —
  illustrative specifics belong in analogy, color-only specifics get cut.
- No em-dashes anywhere.

ALL-FIELDS NON-REPETITION (critical): no scenario, image, fact, or example
may repeat across hook, plain, analogy, and prompt on the same card. Before
finalizing plain, read hook/analogy/prompt side by side — if plain reaches
for the same image or fact any other field already used, find a different
angle even if the wording differs.

THE REPEAT-BACK TEST (the real quality gate): cover the card — could the
reader, five minutes later, explain the actual mechanism, not just repeat a
memorable phrase? If they could only recall an image but not the logic it
carried, the image was decorative, not load-bearing. Fail and rewrite.

BANNED plain patterns:
- "It's not X, it's Y" constructions
- "While X might seem true, Y is actually…"
- Triads of exactly three: "fast, reliable, and efficient" — vary the count
- Sentences all roughly the same length — mix short punchy ones with longer ones
- "Most people don't realize…" / "It's important to note…"
- Rhetorical-question-then-answer pattern ("Why isn't X celebrated? Because Y.")
- "It's X: by the time Y, Z" colon-explainer constructions

BRACKETED TERM RULE: when simplifying would lose a real, widely-used word
worth knowing, preserve it via bracketed clarification rather than dumbing
it down: "(some call this cognitive dissonance)".

Good plain (BATNA — power, 1 sentence, acronym expanded, mechanism intact):
  "Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement — the deal you'd walk straight
  into if this one fell apart. The stronger that backup, the more power you
  hold in the room."

Bad plain (Supernormal Stimuli — 84 words, 5 sentences — too long):
  "Supernormal stimuli refers to an exaggerated version of a stimulus that
  produces a stronger response than the original natural stimulus would. This
  phenomenon occurs because our nervous systems evolved to respond to certain
  cues in our natural environment, but when those cues are artificially
  amplified, our evolved responses go into overdrive..."

===============================================================================
ANALOGY FIELD RULES
===============================================================================

- 1–2 sentences. Concrete, specific, vivid. Varied openers.
- Must pass the 12-year-old test — no abstraction, no jargon.
- Famous person references allowed: "Like when Federer misses an easy shot
  because he's thinking about the last point."
- Concrete beats abstract every time. You must be able to picture it.
- Do NOT use: "It's like a machine that…", "Think of it as a process…",
  "Imagine a system where…"
- Do NOT use "It's not X, it's Y" or "Sure, X works, but Y is the real…"
- Vary the sentence opener — don't start every analogy the same way

Good: "You're more upset losing a €20 note than happy finding one."
Bad:  "It's like how people feel worse about negative outcomes than
      they feel good about equivalently positive ones."

===============================================================================
PROMPT FIELD RULES
===============================================================================

The prompt field is the most-failed field. A great prompt makes a card 10x
more valuable. A lazy prompt undermines the whole concept.

PROMPT TYPE — choose the right type for the concept:

TYPE A — REFLECTION (most common)
  Use for: psychology, identity, relationships, philosophy, health concepts
  Forces the user to surface a specific memory, person, or situation.
  Example: "Name the last time your ego cost you something. What would
  you do differently with two more minutes of pause?"

TYPE B — SCENARIO / ACTION (use for tactical, behavioral, language concepts)
  Use for: business, power, language, thinking concepts with direct application
  Puts the user in a specific situation and asks what they'd do or say.
  Example: "You have 48 hours to test whether demand exists for your idea
  before spending a cent on building it. What's the test?"

TYPE C — CONVERSATION STARTER (use for highly social, shareable concepts)
  Use for: relationships, language, society, status/power concepts
  Gives the user a specific line or question they could use tonight.
  Example: "Next time someone confidently explains something you disagree with,
  try: 'What would have to be true for that to be wrong?' See what happens."

TYPE D — CHALLENGE / PROVOCATION (use for counterintuitive or contrarian concepts)
  Use for: society, thinking, philosophy, tech-ai concepts — where the concept
  challenges an assumption
  Challenges the user to test or confront the concept directly.
  Example: "Write down the three most confident opinions you hold about people
  who are different from you. Now ask: have you actually spent time with them?"

TYPE E — OBSERVATION (use for structural, systems-level, or analytical concepts)
  Use for: science, society, systems-level thinking, tech-ai concepts where
  self-reflection doesn't fit
  Directs attention outward — observe, notice, spot the pattern in the world.
  Example: "In the next 48 hours, notice when someone uses complexity to avoid
  a direct answer. Count how many times it happens."

RULES THAT APPLY TO ALL TYPES:

1. FORCES SPECIFICITY: The user must surface a specific person, decision,
   moment, resource, sentence, or time window. Never "an area of your life."

2. CONCEPT-SPECIFIC: Strip the term and show only the prompt — the reader
   should be able to guess which concept it came from.

3. PERMISSION-GIVING LANGUAGE: Lower the bar. "Smallest, ugliest version"
   beats "minimum viable approach." "What would you actually say" beats
   "consider having the conversation."

4. ANSWERABLE IN UNDER 2 MINUTES. No 10-year-plan prompts.

5. NEVER OPEN WITH: "Have you ever…" / "Think about how this applies…" /
   "Reflect on…" / "Consider the role of…"

6. NO TEMPLATE OVERUSE: No more than 3 prompts per batch may begin with the
   same first 4 words.

ANTI-PATTERNS — REJECT AND REWRITE IF PROMPT:
- Starts with "Where in your life are you…" for the 4th+ time in a batch
- Could fit 3+ different concepts (the swappable-prompt failure)
- Asks the user to "think about" without forcing a concrete answer
- Ends with "...would serve you better?" or similar generic closing
- Uses the structure "In your most important X — are you A or B?"

===============================================================================
SCORING RUBRIC
===============================================================================

universality:
  10 = applies to virtually everyone regardless of profession or culture
  5  = applies to most educated adults
  1  = applies only to a specific narrow group

actionability:
  10 = someone could apply this within 24 hours of learning it
  5  = useful as a mental model but not directly actionable
  1  = purely theoretical or philosophical

novelty:
  10 = counterintuitive, rarely discussed, genuine aha-moment
  5  = familiar but worth articulating precisely
  1  = everyone already knows this

conversation_value:
  10 = using this term correctly would noticeably impress someone intelligent
  5  = useful in the right context but not status-shifting
  1  = common vocabulary anyone could use

Minimum composite for inclusion: 6.0

===============================================================================
SELF-CHECK BEFORE RETURNING OUTPUT
===============================================================================

Run these checks across all extracted prompts as a group:

1. FEYNMAN CHECK: Would a sharp 12-year-old understand every hook, plain,
   and analogy? If any word would make them pause — cut or replace it.

2. HOOK LENGTH: Count words. Target 8-12, hard ceiling 14 — any hook over
   14 words, cut it. Also check: front-loaded trigger word, one dominant
   lever, everyday-language pass, no overlap with plain.

3. PLAIN CHECK: Count characters and words. Any plain over 200 chars / 40 words
   that isn't a genuinely complex concept — compress it. Also check: no
   real-world specific claims (names/dates/dollar figures), no scenario or
   image repeated from hook/analogy/prompt, repeat-back test passes (reader
   could explain the mechanism, not just recall a phrase, five minutes later).

4. TERM LENGTH: Any term outside the 2-5 word range that isn't a coined/named
   concept — rewrite it. Also check: no overlap with hook (2+ shared content
   words or restated angle).

5. OPENER VARIETY: Count first-4-word openings across all prompts. Any phrase
   appearing more than 3 times — rewrite the duplicates.

6. SEMANTIC OVERLAP: Read all prompts in sequence. Any two that could swap
   between concepts and still make sense — rewrite the weaker one.

7. PROMPT TYPE CHECK: For each concept, confirm you chose the right prompt
   type (A/B/C/D/E). A psychology/identity concept getting a Type E
   observation prompt is a mismatch — fix it.

8. CONCRETENESS: Each prompt references at least one specific person,
   decision, time window, or resource. If fully abstract — rewrite.

9. REVERSE TEST: Strip the concept name. Can a reader guess the concept
   from the prompt alone? If not — it's not concept-specific enough.

10. ANTI-SLOP CHECK: Scan every hook, plain, analogy, and prompt for these
    banned patterns and rewrite any that match:
    - Em-dashes (—) anywhere in hook or term
    - "You're not X, you're Y" / "It's not X, it's Y"
    - "While X might seem right, Y is actually…"
    - "Sure, X works. But Y is where the real…"
    - Mid-sentence reveal: "The answer? Simpler than you think."
    - "Most people don't realize…" / "Here's the thing:" / "At the end of the day"
    - Every sentence roughly the same length — vary it
    - Exactly three items in a list — use one, two, or four instead
    - Unearned profundity: "Everything shifted" / "A new era of…" / "game-changing"

11. HOOK TONE-BALANCE CHECK: Across the full batch, is roughly 60% of
    hooks landing straight/specific/blunt and 40% landing funny/twisted/
    surprising? If the batch has collapsed into one register (all blunt,
    or all sarcastic, or all fear-based) — rotate 2-3 hooks to a different
    pattern from the menu, choosing concepts that naturally fit the shift.

If anything fails the self-check, fix it before returning the array.
