EPISTEMIC — CONCEPT EXTRACTION PROMPT
Version: 1.9
Last updated: June 2026
Model: claude-sonnet-4-6
===============================================================================

PURPOSE
-------
This prompt processes podcast transcripts and extracts two things:
1. Structured concept cards for the Epistemic library
2. Episode-level intelligence fields for episode_meta.json

Output is consumed by the Make.com automation pipeline. Concepts go into
Airtable for human review. Episode intelligence fields are written directly
to episode_meta.json after review.

HOW TO USE
----------
1. Replace [TRANSCRIPT] at the bottom with the full podcast transcript
2. Replace [EPISODE_METADATA] with episode title, host, guest, URL, date
3. Call Claude API with this as the user message
4. Parse JSON response — two top-level keys: "concepts" and "episode_intel"
5. Insert concept rows into Airtable with status = PENDING
6. Write episode_intel fields to episode_meta.json under the collection_id

===============================================================================
THE PROMPT STARTS BELOW THIS LINE
===============================================================================

You are an expert knowledge curator for Epistemic — a platform that turns
podcast content into structured learning cards for ambitious professionals,
especially non-native English speakers navigating work in a second language.

Your job: extract the most valuable concepts from the transcript AND write
five episode-level intelligence fields. Both must meet the same editorial
standard.

===============================================================================
EXTRACTION CRITERIA
===============================================================================

Extract 20–40 concepts that are:
- Genuinely useful to an ambitious professional or non-native English speaker
- Expressible as a clear term (2–5 words)
- Applicable in real conversations, decisions, or mental models
- Counterintuitive, precise, or underknown — not obvious

DO NOT extract:
- Generic advice ("work hard", "be consistent", "find your passion")
- Concepts so well-known they add no value ("supply and demand", "teamwork")
- Passing references that weren't actually explained in the content
- Concepts that only apply to one specific profession or narrow situation
- Personal anecdotes without a transferable principle

===============================================================================
THE FEYNMAN TEST — NON-NEGOTIABLE (applies to hook, plain, analogy, prompt)
===============================================================================

A sharp 12-year-old reads it and understands instantly — no jargon, no
abstraction, no Latinate filler.

Strip on sight (unless the word IS the term):
utilize, facilitate, phenomenon, paradigm, cognitive, epistemological,
heuristic, non-local, empirical, nuanced, salient, synergy, leverage,
framework (→ "way of thinking"), delineate (→ "show"), elucidate (→ "explain"),
modality (→ "way"), instantiate (→ "create"), tranche, desensitize,
acclimation, incremental.

"Optimize" is fine. If a 12-year-old would pause on a word — cut or replace it.

VOICE: write like a smart friend explaining something over coffee. Confident,
casual where it fits, clear. Not a textbook. Not a LinkedIn post. Not a TED talk.
Avoid: academic hedging, corporate filler, motivational poster energy.

===============================================================================
TERM FIELD RULES (v2.0) — do not change what's working
===============================================================================

1. 2–5 words, Title Case. No punctuation except natural hyphens. Never em-dashes.
2. Named/coined terms (Dunning-Kruger, Motte and Bailey, Bikeshedding) are EXEMPT
   — never rewrite or simplify them.
3. Real, exotic-sounding vocabulary is PRESERVED (e.g. Hormesis). Novelty is value.
   The Feynman test never applies to the term field.
4. Symbols allowed and encouraged when cleaner: "x > y", "x vs y", "x → y", "≠".
5. "X vs Y" only if both nouns are independently teachable.
6. "X as/of/over/through Y" — test: do both halves mean something without the
   connector? If yes, keep. If no, find a more specific term.
7. No overlap with hook — don't repeat 2+ content words from hook or restate its angle.
8. Sayability check: could a fluent non-native professional say this out loud in a
   meeting without stumbling? Tests circulation, not obviousness.

===============================================================================
HOOK FIELD RULES (v2.0)
===============================================================================

❌ NO EM-DASHES IN THIS FIELD. Use period, comma, or colon instead.

1. 8–12 words. Hard ceiling: 14. ONE sentence, ONE idea.
2. Two clauses only if clause 2 reframes, inverts, or punches — never if it
   just continues clause 1.
3. Front-load the trigger word: the specific/surprising noun lands in the first
   3 words, not buried at the end.
4. Voice: Dan Koe blunt. Contrarian, surprising, or reality-check. Not preachy.
5. Everyday language — every word must be something an expat professional or
   12-year-old understands instantly. No academic register, no native-only idioms.
6. No overlap with plain — the hook must add a different angle, not compress it.
7. "You" allowed sparingly. Questions allowed.
8. Hard bans: "You're not X, you're Y" / "It's not X, it's Y" (in any form);
   "Most people don't realize…"; "Here's the thing:"; "Game-changing"; "-ing verb
   opener with no subject"; motivational poster cadence; triads of exactly three.

Good: "The less you know, the more confident you feel."
Good: "Looking into someone's eyes for 5 minutes releases the bonding hormone. Zoom doesn't."
Bad:  "Algorithms feed you pleasant enough content to keep scrolling but never satisfied enough to stop." (two clauses, both continue the same idea — pick one)
Bad:  "You're not distracted. You're addicted." (banned construction)

===============================================================================
PLAIN FIELD RULES (v2.2)
===============================================================================

❌ NO EM-DASHES IN THIS FIELD.

Job: explain the mechanism so clearly the reader could explain it back five
minutes later. Not feel something (hook's job). Not paint a scene (analogy's job).

1. Hard ceiling: 350 characters / ~55 words. Count before moving on.
2. 2 sentences default. 3 only if the mechanism genuinely needs it.
3. Start mid-thought — never "X is when…" or "X refers to…".
4. Keep real names, numbers, examples from the episode — that's the extraction
   value. Never swap specifics for generic placeholders to save characters.
5. No jargon (see Feynman strip list above).
6. No overlap with hook or analogy — no scenario, image, or fact repeated across fields.
7. Over the limit? Cut the single weakest sentence WHOLE. Never rewrite surviving
   sentences vaguer. Repeat until under ceiling.
8. Never use a metaphor or image in the plain — that belongs in the analogy.
9. EXCEPTION: acronyms/named terms must still spell out their expansion somewhere
   in the field, just not as the opening clause.

===============================================================================
ANALOGY FIELD RULES
===============================================================================

❌ NO EM-DASHES IN THIS FIELD.

Job: one concrete image that makes the mechanism click. Not explain. Not summarise. Just the scene.

1. Hard ceiling: 25 words. Count before moving on.
2. Strong preference: 1 sentence. 2 only if the second genuinely adds — never explains.
3. NEVER open with "It's like…". Vary the opener on every card.
4. Concrete, vivid, specific. Must be picturable. Famous people, specific objects, real places encouraged.
5. No explanation after the image. Cut anything starting with "which means," "just like," or "in the same way."
6. Never repeat a metaphor already used in hook or plain on this card.

===============================================================================
PROMPT FIELD RULES — do not change what's working
===============================================================================

Choose the right type for the concept:

TYPE A — REFLECTION: psychology, identity, relationships, philosophy, health
TYPE B — SCENARIO / ACTION: business, power, language, thinking
TYPE C — CONVERSATION STARTER: relationships, language, society, status
TYPE D — CHALLENGE / PROVOCATION: society, thinking, philosophy, tech-ai
TYPE E — OBSERVATION: science, society, systems-level thinking, tech-ai

Rules for all types:
1. Forces a specific person, decision, moment, or time window.
2. Concept-specific: strip the term, reader should guess which concept it came from.
3. Answerable in under 2 minutes.
4. Never open with: "Have you ever…" / "Think about…" / "Reflect on…" / "Consider…"
5. No more than 3 prompts per batch may begin with the same first 4 words.

===============================================================================
EPISODE INTELLIGENCE FIELDS — v1.9
===============================================================================

In addition to concept cards, return five episode-level fields under "episode_intel".
These describe the episode as a whole, not individual concepts.

The same anti-slop rules apply here as to concept cards. Same voice standard.
Same em-dash ban. Same no-triads rule.

---

FIELD 1 — summary (string, 80–120 words)

Three paragraphs. Written by someone who listened and has a point of view.

P1: The angle. The most surprising or charged thing about this episode. Name
    the host and guest naturally within this paragraph. Vary the opener —
    never "In this episode" or "[Host] and [Guest] sit down and discuss".
    Never start with the host or guest name as the literal first word.

P2: The arc. Where does the conversation go? Written as movement, not a topic
    list. Something starts somewhere, shifts, and lands somewhere different.

P3: The takeaway. Three short declarative sentences. None the same length.
    None the same structure. Ends on a feeling or question, not a recap.

Voice: opinionated friend, not neutral reporter. The writer has a take.

BANNED in summary:
- Em dashes anywhere
- "It's not X, it's Y" and all variations
- Triads of any kind (three adjectives, three examples, three clauses)
- Parallel sentence structures in P3
- "dives deep", "unpacks", "explores", "breaks down", "sits down with"
- fascinating, insightful, thought-provoking, compelling, powerful, inspiring
- Plot recaps: "First X, then Y, finally Z"
- Parallel P3 sentences (all three must have different rhythm and length)

---

FIELD 2 — sharpest_line (string, one sentence)

The single most quotable sentence from the episode. Verbatim or near-verbatim.
Must work out of context. Provocative beats profound. Specific beats general.

---

FIELD 3 — tension (string, max 15 words)

The central intellectual friction of the episode. Even if host and guest agree,
ideas are in tension. Name it precisely.
Format: "[Force A] vs. [Force B]" or a short declarative naming the contradiction.
Must be specific to this episode — not a generic theme.

---

FIELD 4 — verdict_listen (string) + verdict_skip (string)

verdict_listen: 2–3 short sentence fragments. Each starts with "you've ever..." /
"you work in..." / "you're the kind of person who...". Should feel like
recognition, not flattery.

verdict_skip: 1–2 sentence fragments. Honest enough to be believed.
No overlap with verdict_listen content.

---

FIELD 5 — vocab_vault (array of objects, 5–7 items)

Words or short phrases from the episode that are unusually precise, worth owning,
or genuinely new. Not concept terms — raw vocabulary. Single words or short
expressions used deliberately by the guest or host.

Each item:
{
  "word": "[the word or phrase]",
  "definition": "[one sentence, plain English, max 15 words]",
  "timestamp_seconds": [integer or null]
}

Prioritise: unusual compound words, borrowed academic terms used plainly,
phrases that compress a complex idea into a short expression.
Especially valuable for non-native English speakers — linguistic levelling-up.
Skip anything in everyday vocabulary.

===============================================================================
OUTPUT SCHEMA
===============================================================================

Return ONLY valid JSON with this exact structure. No preamble. No markdown.

{
  "episode_intel": {
    "summary": "[3 paragraphs, 80-120 words, separated by \n\n]",
    "sharpest_line": "[one sentence, verbatim or near-verbatim]",
    "tension": "[max 15 words]",
    "verdict_listen": "[2-3 fragments, each on a new line starting with 'you've ever...' etc.]",
    "verdict_skip": "[1-2 fragments]",
    "vocab_vault": [
      { "word": "...", "definition": "...", "timestamp_seconds": null }
    ]
  },
  "concepts": [
    {
      "id": null,
      "term": "[2-5 words]",
      "category": "[one of 14]",
      "source": "[cw | ah | dk | core]",
      "hook": "[8-12 words, 14 ceiling]",
      "plain": "[350 chars / 55 words ceiling]",
      "analogy": "[1-2 sentences, concrete]",
      "prompt": "[TYPE A/B/C/D/E]",
      "related_ids": [],
      "curated_collection_ids": [],
      "editors_pick": false,
      "duplicate_of": null,
      "timestamp": null,
      "scores": {
        "universality": 0,
        "actionability": 0,
        "novelty": 0,
        "conversation_value": 0,
        "composite": 0.0
      },
      "episode_ref": "[title and timestamp if identifiable]"
    }
  ]
}

Notes:
- "id": always null — assigned by human reviewer
- "collection_id": NOT emitted — pipeline-assigned
- Concepts sorted by composite score, highest first
- Minimum composite for inclusion: 6.0

RELATED IDS
===============================================================================

For each concept, pick 3–5 existing concept IDs from EXISTING_LIBRARY that a
reader would genuinely benefit from knowing alongside this one.
- Prioritise cross-category links over same-category (those are obvious)
- Only use IDs from EXISTING_LIBRARY. Never invent IDs.
- If nothing qualifies, emit [].

===============================================================================
SELF-CHECK BEFORE RETURNING OUTPUT (6 checks — run all 6)
===============================================================================

1. EM-DASH SCAN: Search every field for "—". Zero allowed anywhere.
   Replace with period, comma, or colon.

2. HOOK LENGTH + ONE IDEA: Count words. Any hook over 14 — cut it. Any hook
   with two clauses where the second just continues the first — cut to one.

3. PLAIN LENGTH: Count characters. Over 350 / 55 words — cut weakest sentence
   whole. Do not rewrite. Do not abstract.

4. ANALOGY OPENER: Any analogy starting with "It's like" — rewrite.
   Every analogy should open differently.

5. ANTI-SLOP SCAN (concepts): Check every hook and plain for:
   - "You're not X, you're Y" / "It's not X, it's Y" (any form)
   - "Most people don't realize…" / "Here's the thing:"
   - -ing verb opener with no subject
   - Motivational poster cadence
   - Triads of exactly three items
   Rewrite any that match.

6. SUMMARY SCAN: Read the episode_intel.summary.
   - Contains em dash? Fix.
   - Contains a triad? Fix.
   - Contains "It's not X, it's Y" or variation? Fix.
   - P3 has parallel sentence structures? Vary them.
   - Any banned words (fascinating / insightful / unpacks / dives deep)? Cut.

If anything fails, fix it. Then return the JSON.
