# Epistemic Analogy Style Guide — v1.0
**Scope:** The `analogy` field only
**Last updated:** June 2026 (first edition, aligned with extraction prompt v1.8)
**Companion docs:** hook-style-guide.md, plain-style-guide.md, term-style-guide.md, prompt-style-guide.md

---

## What the analogy has to do

Make the mechanism click through a single concrete image. Not explain. Not summarise. Not repeat what the plain already said. Just one scene that transfers the logic instantly — so the reader gets it even if they forgot the plain.

The analogy is the only field allowed to use metaphor and image. If you find yourself writing a metaphor in the plain or hook, move it here.

---

## Hard rules

**Length**
- Hard ceiling: 25 words. Count before moving on.
- Strong preference: 1 sentence. Land the whole thing in one clear image.
- 2 sentences allowed only if the second genuinely adds a new angle — never if it explains the first.

**Opener**
- NEVER start with "It's like…" — banned unconditionally.
- NEVER start with "Think of it as…" / "Imagine…" / "Picture…" — banned.
- Vary the opener on every card. No two analogies in a batch should start the same way.
- Good openers: a specific noun, a proper name, a concrete object, a verb that places you immediately in the scene.

**Content**
- Concrete, vivid, specific. Must be picturable. If you can't see it, it's too abstract — cut.
- Famous people, specific objects, real places, brand names: all fine and actively encouraged.
- No explanation after the image. The scene speaks for itself. Cut any sentence starting with "which means," "just like," "in the same way," or "this is why."
- No abstract comparisons: "the difference between X and Y" without a specific concrete scene is banned.
- Never repeat a metaphor or image already used in the hook or plain on the same card.

**Em-dashes**
- ❌ Banned unconditionally. Replace with colon, period, or comma.

---

## What makes a good analogy

The best analogies share three qualities:
1. **Specific** — not "a tool" but "a scalpel"; not "a vehicle" but "a getaway car"
2. **Transfer the logic exactly** — the relationship in the analogy mirrors the mechanism in the concept. If it only feels similar but the logic doesn't map, find another one.
3. **Self-contained** — the reader gets it without having read the plain. If they need context to understand the analogy, it's not working as one.

---

## Good examples

"You're more upset losing a €20 note than happy finding one." *(19 words, 1 sentence, specific, no opener)*

"A plant kept indoors forever never develops strong roots." *(9 words, 1 sentence, picturable, immediate)*

"Tuning a guitar out of tune first thing: every song after sounds wrong." *(13 words, no explanation appended)*

"Thinking marriage will solve your loneliness: the wedding day is great, then you wake up the next morning still you." *(20 words, 2 sentences but both load-bearing)*

---

## Bad examples

"It's like being on a cruise ship that circles the same route forever — plenty of entertainment, no real destination, and you forget what solid ground feels like."
❌ "It's like" opener. Em-dash. 31 words. Explanation appended.

"It's like the difference between smelling fresh bread and looking at a photo of bread. One makes you feel something. The other is just information."
❌ "It's like" opener. Abstract comparison. Explanation sentences. 26 words.

"You can think of it as a system where inputs that were once rewarding gradually stop producing the same level of output, requiring ever-larger inputs to achieve the same effect."
❌ No opener variety. No concrete image. 32 words. Pure explanation.

---

## Self-check before finalising

1. Is it under 25 words?
2. Does it open with "It's like," "Think of," or "Imagine"? If yes — rewrite.
3. Is there a sentence after the image that explains it? If yes — cut it.
4. Can you picture it? If no — find a more concrete scene.
5. Does the image or metaphor appear anywhere in the hook or plain on this card? If yes — find a different one.
