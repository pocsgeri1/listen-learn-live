# The Curator's Manifesto
## What Gergely's Taste Tells Us About Epistemic — And What Epistemic Is Really About

*Private. For Gergely and future collaborators.*

---

There's a moment in every great podcast episode when the guest says something and you think: *I've always felt this but I've never had the words for it.* That feeling — the simultaneous recognition and acquisition of language for a thing you already knew — is the closest thing to intellectual joy. It feels like being handed a key to a door you didn't know was locked.

Gergely Pocs has been chasing that feeling for years. And then he did something unusual: he decided to bottle it.

## The Playlist as Personality X-Ray

Start with the podcasts. Not as product decisions — as personality data.

Modern Wisdom. Diary of a CEO. Lex Fridman. Dan Koe.

Look at what these four men have in common. None of them are academics. All of them are rigorous. None of them are purely theoretical. All of them are obsessed with the mechanism underneath the behaviour — *why* people do what they do, not just that they do it. All four built audiences by refusing to choose between intellectual depth and practical relevance. They live exactly at that intersection.

Now look at what separates them. Williamson goes evolutionary. Bartlett goes emotional. Fridman goes philosophical. Koe goes existential. Together they cover the full topology of a certain kind of mind: one that wants to understand humans in their totality — biologically, psychologically, philosophically, economically — without reducing them to any one of those dimensions.

This is not a random playlist. This is a map of a worldview.

Gergely doesn't curate podcasts. He curates *lenses*. And the through-line across all four is identical: **ideas that change behaviour, not just beliefs.**

## The Man Behind the Curation

You don't build something like Epistemic unless you have personally experienced the problem it solves.

Gergely is Hungarian. He thinks in one language and operates in another. He's been in rooms — boardrooms, dinner parties, conversations — where someone says something referencing a concept he half-knows, and he nods, and he processes it later, and two days later he has the articulate understanding he needed in the moment. Two days too late.

That's the gap. Not comprehension — translation. Not intelligence — fluency. The ability to not just understand an idea but to *own* it. To deploy it. To build on it in real time in the language of the conversation you're actually in.

Every ambitious non-native English professional knows this feeling. Almost none of them would admit it.

Here's what's remarkable: Gergely didn't build Epistemic for the market. He built it for himself. The market just turned out to be enormous.

## What Epistemic Is Actually About

On the surface: a podcast learning platform. Concept cards. Plain definitions. Memorable analogies.

Under the surface: a belonging machine.

The word *epistemic* means relating to knowledge or the conditions for acquiring it. Gergely chose it deliberately. But the product isn't really about acquiring knowledge. It's about acquiring *confidence*. The confidence to walk into any intellectual conversation and contribute — not just follow.

There's a specific kind of imposter syndrome that non-native English speakers experience that almost never gets named. It's invisible because it looks like intelligence — you follow the conversation — but it feels like inadequacy — you can't quite lead it. You understand Nassim Taleb when you read him but you can't casually reference "antifragility" in a meeting without sounding like you just Googled it. You've listened to fifty hours of Chris Williamson but you wouldn't say "Dunning-Kruger" in a pitch without second-guessing yourself.

Epistemic closes that gap. It takes the ideas that matter and makes them *yours* — not just understood, but owned. It gives you the etymology, the analogy, the conversational handle. It makes you feel at home in the discourse.

That's not a learning app. That's an identity app. A very quiet, very confident, very dignified identity app.

## The Taste as Product Philosophy

Gergely's curation choices encode a product philosophy.

He picks podcasts where the host is intellectually honest about complexity. He doesn't pick podcasts that give you "five steps to success." He picks podcasts where the guest says "actually, I'm not sure — but here's how I think about it." That epistemic humility — the willingness to hold an idea with precision but not with certainty — is baked into how Epistemic presents concepts. The cards don't declare. They illuminate. The prompt at the end isn't "this is what you should do" — it's "here's a question worth sitting with."

He picks podcasts where the guest has *done something*. Lex is a scientist and interviewer. Bartlett built a company, burned out, and rebuilt. Koe has lived the thing he talks about. Williamson trains seriously and thinks seriously. There's no distance between the ideas and the lives of the people holding them. This is why Epistemic's analogies are always grounded — concrete scenarios, not abstract illustrations. Gergely instinctively mistrusts ideas that float above the evidence.

He picks podcasts with high signal-to-noise. Hours-long conversations where the interesting stuff is spread throughout, not packaged in neat summaries. This is the problem Epistemic solves — not just for non-native speakers, but for anyone who doesn't have three hours to find the needle in the hay. The curation IS the product. Not the technology. Not the interface. The editorial judgement.

## The Specific Person Epistemic Is For

Not every expat. Not every non-native English speaker. Not every podcast listener.

The person who moved countries not because they had to, but because they were restless in ways their home culture couldn't quite contain. Who is ambitious in a way that doesn't map neatly onto their origin story. Who has a harder time explaining what they do to their parents than to a stranger at a conference. Who reads in English even though it's harder — because the books they want to read exist only in English. Who has opinions about ideas, not just about things.

This person already listens to the podcasts. They already want the concepts. They just need someone to do the work of making those concepts portable — card-sized, sticky, deployable in the moment that matters.

Gergely is that someone. Because he is that person.

## Why This Has a Moat

Not the technology. The taste.

The concept library Gergely is building is not replicable by an algorithm. It requires someone who knows which ideas are actually useful versus which ideas just sound impressive. Someone who can tell the difference between a concept that will change how you think and a concept that will change how you sound for one dinner party. Someone who has personally tested these ideas against the friction of real conversations in a second language.

That's editorial judgement. It takes years to develop and it's almost impossible to fake. A language model can generate concept cards. Spotify could build this feature tomorrow. Neither of them has Gergely's taste.

The moat isn't the product. The moat is the person behind it.
