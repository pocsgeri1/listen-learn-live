## What the prompt has to do

Force the reader to apply the concept today — not "think about it" but actually do something specific with it. The best prompts make the concept personal before the reader has time to intellectualise it.

Test: strip the term from the card. Could the reader guess which concept this prompt belongs to? If yes, it's concept-specific enough. If no, rewrite.

---

## The five types

Choose the type that fits the concept's category and mechanism:

**TYPE A — REFLECTION**
Best for: psychology, identity, relationships, philosophy, health
Forces a specific memory, person, or pattern — not a general life area.
> "Name the last time your ego cost you something. What would you do differently?"

**TYPE B — SCENARIO / ACTION**
Best for: business, power, language, thinking
Forces a specific test, decision, or experiment within 48 hours.
> "You have 48 hours to test demand for your idea before spending a cent. What's the test?"

**TYPE C — CONVERSATION STARTER**
Best for: relationships, language, society, status
Gives the reader an exact line to try in a real conversation.
> "Next time someone confidently explains something you disagree with, try: 'What would have to be true for that to be wrong?' See what happens."

**TYPE D — CHALLENGE / PROVOCATION**
Best for: society, thinking, philosophy, tech-ai
Challenges a belief the reader almost certainly holds.
> "Write down three confident opinions you hold about people different from you. Have you actually spent time with them?"

**TYPE E — OBSERVATION**
Best for: science, society, systems-level thinking, tech-ai
Asks the reader to notice something in the next 24-48 hours.
> "In the next 48 hours, notice when someone uses complexity to avoid a direct answer."

## Hard rules

**Specificity**
- Forces a specific person, decision, moment, or time window — never "an area of your life," "your relationships," or "your work."
- Answerable in under 2 minutes.
- Concept-specific: the concept's core mechanism should be visible in the prompt even without the term.

**Banned openers**
- "Have you ever…"
- "Think about…" / "Think of…"
- "Reflect on…"
- "Consider…"
- "Ask yourself…" (borderline — use sparingly, never as default)

**Batch rule**
- No more than 3 prompts in a batch of 20 may begin with the same first 4 words.

**Length**
- No hard word ceiling, but prompts that run over 40 words usually contain a second question or an unnecessary setup. Cut to one sharp ask.

**Voice**
- Direct. Not therapeutic. Not preachy. A friend asking a sharp question, not a coach assigning homework.
- Second person ("you," "your") is the default register.
- Casual language allowed — match the register of the hook.

## Good examples

"Name one personality trait you dislike and blame on your parents. Now flip it: what strength in your life came from the exact same source?" *(TYPE A, specific, concept-traceable)*

"Block 4 days on your calendar in the next 3 months. Go somewhere without your phone. Notice what happens." *(TYPE B, specific action, time-bounded)*

"Name the last big goal you hit. How many days did the satisfaction last before you started chasing the next thing?" *(TYPE A, forces a real memory, not a general reflection)*

---

## Bad examples

"Think about how meaning shows up in your life and consider whether your daily habits support it."
❌ "Think about" opener. Too broad. Not concept-specific. No specific moment or person forced.

"Have you ever noticed how technology affects your relationships? Reflect on this."
❌ "Have you ever" opener. "Reflect on" opener. No specific action. Not answerable in 2 minutes.

"Ask yourself: am I living in alignment with my values?"
❌ Could belong to any self-help concept ever written. Not concept-specific.

---

## Self-check before finalising

1. Does it open with "Have you ever," "Think about," "Reflect on," or "Consider"? If yes — rewrite.
2. Does it force a specific person, moment, or time window? If no — add one.
3. Strip the term. Can you guess the concept from the prompt alone? If no — sharpen.
4. Is it answerable in under 2 minutes? If no — cut the second question.
5. Is it under 40 words? If over — cut the setup, keep the ask.
